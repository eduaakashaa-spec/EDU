What this chapter teaches

The 28 Arabic letters — the foundation of everything you will read, write or pronounce.

Arabic has 28 letters, all consonants. They flow right to left and most of them join up like cursive English. Each letter has a name (like “Baa”) and a sound (like “b”). Some letters look similar but differ by tiny dots above or below — pay attention to those dots. Once you know the 28 letters, you can read any Arabic word, even before you understand its meaning.

  • Arabic is written and read right to left — even your eyes need to retrain.
  • Most letters change shape slightly when they join others (initial, medial, final).
  • Dots above or below distinguish letters with the same base shape (ب ت ث).
  • There are no capital letters in Arabic. One form fits all positions in a sentence.

FAQ · For Parents & Learners Honest answers to the questions we hear most
Q·01 · For Students — I have never seen Arabic before. Where do I start?

Begin with Chapter 01 (the alphabet) and don’t skip ahead. Spend ten quiet minutes per chapter. Use the Listen mode first, then the Writing mode. Your eyes need to retrain to read right-to-left — go gently with yourself.

Q·02 · For Parents — How long does this beginner programme take to complete?

Most beginners finish all nine chapters in three to four weeks at fifteen minutes a day. Younger children may need six to eight weeks. We deliberately keep each chapter small and reviewable, so coming back to it builds confidence rather than pressure.

Q·03 · For Parents — Do I need to know Arabic to support my child?

No. Every chapter has a written explanation in plain English, audio narration, and a clear written tip for what to practise. The progress report (further down this page) is designed to be readable by a parent who has never studied Arabic.

Q·04 · For Students — My handwriting looks nothing like the example. Is that bad?

Not at all. Arabic calligraphy takes years to master. The Writing handbook on this page shows the basic stroke direction; what matters at this stage is that the letter is recognisable and that the dots are in the right place. Speed comes later; accuracy comes first.

Q·05 · For Everyone — Why do some letters change shape when I see them in books?

Arabic letters connect like cursive English. Most letters have an isolated, initial, medial and final form — small variations of the same shape. We focus on the isolated form first, since that’s the foundation. Connected forms are introduced in our Level 02 programme.

Q·06 · For Students — I keep mixing up similar letters like ب ت ث. Help?

You are not alone — this is the most common beginner challenge. The trick is to say the dot count aloud as you write: “Baa, one dot below”; “Taa, two dots above”; “Thaa, three dots above”. The body of the letter is identical; the dots tell the whole story.
